Problem 1. Slough Soil Salinity

a.

Pearson’s correlation and Spearman rank correlation are appropriate tests to determine the strength of the relationship between Salinity and California pickleweed biomass because correlation can be used independently of a linear model because the 2 variables are related to each other. Pearson’s correlation is a parametric test that would show the linear relationship between slough soil salinity and pickleweed biomass. Spearman rank correlation is a non-parametric test that would show the monotonic relationship between slough soil salinity and pickleweed biomass.

The test I ran was the Spearman rank correlation. The assumptions I checked was if there were 1) independent observations and 2) a monotonic relationship between variables. The independent observations in the problem were the slough soil salinity and the pickleweed biomass. The monotonic relationship can be seen in the scatterplot where the dots are following a consistent direction of a curve going up. Since the data is related to each and it aligns with our assumptions, Spearman rank correlation was a appropriate test.

d.

To evaluate the strength of the relationship between pickleweed biomass and soil salinity, I used a Spearman rank correlation test. Based off the test, we found a moderate relationship between pickleweed biomass and soil salinity ( Spearman ρ = 0,59, S = 824, p = 0.003, α = 0.05).

e.

Based of the results of the test, we can conclude that the pickleweed biomass increases when the solute soil salinity increases. The graph shows that the data moves in a upward curve as both mS/cm increases when g increases. If we want to restore the population of pickleweed, we should plant them in soil with higher salinity.
